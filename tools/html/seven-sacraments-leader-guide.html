The Seven Sacraments

Leader Guide — Teaching Notes and Discussion Prompts

Introduction for the Facilitator

The seven sacraments (or holy mysteries) are the means by which God grants us grace through the Church. They were instituted by Christ and entrusted to His apostles. In the Coptic Orthodox tradition, these sacraments mark every stage of the Christian life — from birth in Baptism to the final anointing of the sick.

This guide provides background on each sacrament to help you lead meaningful discussions during or after the game. You do not need to cover every point — let the children's questions guide the conversation.

Note: The sacraments are mysteries, not lessons to be fully explained. It is perfectly fine to say "we don't fully understand how God works through this — that's why we call it a mystery."

Baptism

What it is: The sacrament of new birth. Through three immersions in water, we die with Christ and rise with Him to new life. Original sin is washed away and we become members of the Body of Christ.

Biblical basis: Christ's baptism in the Jordan (Matthew 3:13-17); "Unless one is born of water and the Spirit" (John 3:5); the Great Commission (Matthew 28:19).

Coptic practice: Infants are baptised at 40 days (boys) or 80 days (girls). The priest performs three full immersions. The catechumen is anointed with Ghaliloun oil before baptism and clothed in white afterward.

Discussion prompt: "What does it mean that we 'die and rise' with Christ in baptism? How is your life different because you are baptised?"

Chrismation (Confirmation)

What it is: The sacrament of the seal of the Holy Spirit. Immediately after baptism, the newly baptised is anointed with Holy Myron (sacred chrism), receiving the gift of the Holy Spirit.

Biblical basis: The apostles laid hands on the baptised to receive the Spirit (Acts 8:14-17; 19:6); "You have an anointing from the Holy One" (1 John 2:20).

Coptic practice: The priest anoints 36 parts of the body with Holy Myron, saying "the seal of the gift of the Holy Spirit" at each anointing. The Myron is consecrated by the Pope and contains ingredients tracing back to the apostles.

Discussion prompt: "The Holy Spirit lives in you since your chrismation. How can you be more aware of the Spirit's presence in your daily life?"

Confession (Repentance)

What it is: The sacrament of reconciliation. When we sin after baptism, confession restores our relationship with God. We confess our sins to God before the priest, who grants absolution by the authority Christ gave to His apostles.

Biblical basis: "If we confess our sins, He is faithful and just to forgive us" (1 John 1:9); Christ gave the apostles authority to forgive sins (John 20:22-23).

Coptic practice: Confession is made to a priest (the father of confession) who serves as a witness and guide. The priest places the epitrachelion over the penitent and prays the absolution. Regular confession is expected before receiving Communion.

Discussion prompt: "Why do you think God asks us to confess to a priest instead of only praying privately? What does it mean to have a father of confession?"

Eucharist (Holy Communion)

What it is: The sacrament of sacraments. Bread and wine are transformed into the true Body and Blood of Christ through the Holy Spirit. When we receive Communion, we receive Christ Himself.

Biblical basis: The Last Supper (Matthew 26:26-28; Luke 22:19-20); "Unless you eat the flesh of the Son of Man and drink His blood, you have no life in you" (John 6:53).

Coptic practice: The Divine Liturgy is celebrated by a priest using leavened bread (the Lamb) and wine. The Epiclesis invokes the Holy Spirit. The Fraction prayer breaks the bread. Communicants must fast, confess, and prepare themselves.

Discussion prompt: "When you receive Communion, you receive Jesus Christ Himself. How should that change the way you prepare for the liturgy?"

Anointing of the Sick

What it is: The sacrament of healing. When a person is seriously ill, the Church anoints them with holy oil and prays for God's healing mercy — for body and soul.

Biblical basis: "Is anyone among you sick? Let him call for the elders of the church, and let them pray over him, anointing him with oil" (James 5:14-15); Christ healed the sick throughout His ministry.

Coptic practice: The Sacrament of the Sick (Kandeel) includes seven prayers with readings from the Epistles and Gospels. The priest anoints the sick person's senses with oil and lays hands on them. The prayers ask for forgiveness of sins alongside physical healing.

Discussion prompt: "Why does the Church pray for forgiveness of sins during the anointing of the sick? What is the connection between spiritual and physical health?"

Matrimony (Holy Marriage)

What it is: The sacrament that unites a man and woman in Christ. Marriage in the Church is not merely a contract but a mystery — the couple becomes an icon of Christ and His Church.

Biblical basis: "For this reason a man shall leave his father and mother and be joined to his wife, and the two shall become one flesh. This is a great mystery" (Ephesians 5:31-32); the wedding at Cana (John 2:1-11).

Coptic practice: The wedding includes the betrothal (exchange of rings) and the crowning. The crowns signify that the couple are king and queen of their household. Psalms of joy are chanted. Marriage is a lifelong covenant — the Coptic Church does not recognise divorce except in specific circumstances.

Discussion prompt: "Why do you think the Church crowns the bride and groom? What does it mean to be the 'king and queen' of a Christian home?"

Priesthood (Holy Orders)

What it is: The sacrament by which men are ordained to serve the Church as deacons, priests, or bishops. Through ordination, they receive the grace to celebrate the sacraments, teach, and shepherd God's people.

Biblical basis: Christ chose the twelve apostles (Luke 6:12-16); the apostles ordained others by laying on of hands (Acts 6:6; 1 Timothy 4:14; 2 Timothy 1:6).

Coptic practice: Only a bishop can ordain. The bishop lays hands on the ordinand and breathes on him, as Christ breathed on the apostles. The new priest is vested in his priestly garments. The Coptic Church maintains apostolic succession — an unbroken chain of ordination from St. Mark the Evangelist to the present day.

Discussion prompt: "The priest serves at the altar on behalf of all the people. How can you pray for and support your priest?"

Closing Prayer

Lord Jesus Christ, You established the holy mysteries for the life and salvation of Your people.

Through Baptism You gave us new birth. Through Chrismation You sealed us with Your Spirit. Through Confession You restore us when we fall. Through the Eucharist You feed us with Your own Body and Blood. Through Anointing You heal our sickness. Through Matrimony You bless the union of husband and wife. Through Priesthood You shepherd Your flock.

Help these children to treasure the sacraments and to receive them with reverence, faith, and joy all the days of their lives.

Through the intercessions of the Theotokos, St. Mary, and all the saints. Amen.
