The Narrow Way

Leader Guide — Patristic Reflections and Discussion Prompts

Introduction for Leaders

The Narrow Way is designed for teens but works with any group willing to engage honestly with moral choices. The push-your-luck mechanic creates real tension — and that tension mirrors the actual experience of choosing faithfulness over comfort.

Your role as facilitator is to guide the post-game discussion. The game creates the experience; you help the group process it.

Key principle: Do not moralize during the game. Let the mechanics speak. Save the teaching for the discussion afterward.

Connecting to the Coptic Tradition

The Ascetic Tradition

The "narrow way" is the central image of Coptic ascetic spirituality. Fasting, prayer, vigil, confession, and self-denial are not punishments — they are the narrow gate that leads to genuine freedom. The desert fathers chose the narrow way not because they hated the world, but because they loved God more.

St. Anthony the Great said: "I no longer fear God, but I love Him. For love casts out fear." The narrow way begins in fear but ends in love.

Martyrdom as the Ultimate Narrow Way

The Coptic Church is the Church of martyrs. From the first century to 2015, Copts have chosen the narrow way unto death. The twenty-one Martyrs of Libya whispered "Lord Jesus Christ" as they died. This is the narrow way at its most extreme — and most beautiful.

Repentance: Getting Up After Falling

The "Fall" card in the game costs you progress but does not end the game. This directly mirrors the desert teaching: Abba Sisoes said, "Get up again and again... for a person presents himself to God in the state in which he is found." Falling is not failure. Staying down is.

Discussion Guide by Situation

Speech and Gossip (Cards 1, 6)

The desert fathers considered gossip one of the most destructive sins. Abba Hyperechius said: "It is better to eat meat and drink wine than to eat the flesh of your brothers through slander." Ask: "Why is gossip so easy? What makes it hard to stop?"

Fasting (Card 3)

In Coptic practice, fasting is not optional — it is a gift. The game presents the social pressure to break the fast. Ask: "What does fasting train in us? What happens when we fast cheerfully vs. grudgingly?"

Confession (Card 8)

The sacrament of confession is central to Coptic spiritual life. The "broad way" of hiding is exactly what keeps people from the healing of confession. Ask: "What makes honesty in confession so hard? What does it feel like after honest confession?"

Digital Life (Cards 5, 9)

Screen time and social media comparison are the narrow-way challenges of our generation. The desert fathers fled distraction; we carry it in our pockets. Ask: "What would a 'digital fast' look like for you?"

Pride and Humility (Cards 7, 17, Trial: Pride)

The Pride trial card is the game's sharpest teaching moment: even choosing the narrow way can become a source of pride. St. Macarius said the only thing that defeats the devil is humility. Ask: "Have you ever felt proud of being more spiritual than someone else? What happened?"

After the Game: Closing Reflection

Ask each player to name:

  1. The situation that felt most real to them
  2. One narrow-way choice they want to make this week
  3. One person they can walk the narrow way with (the "Companion" principle)

Close with a prayer. Suggested:

Lord Jesus Christ, You walked the narrowest way of all — the way of the Cross. Give us courage to follow You, not seeking the broad way of comfort but the narrow way of love.

When we fall, help us get up. When we are mocked, give us peace. When we are tired, renew our strength.

Through the prayers of our holy fathers, the desert saints, and all the martyrs who chose Your way unto death — keep us faithful. Amen.
